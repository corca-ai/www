Axhibit: The moment when a design review becomes a judgment of productability

Axhibit's product direction summarized in a live interview with Song Ki-seong. A story about a tool that allows experts to identify problems, the virtual public to gather responses, and gauge the potential of advertising design earlier.

Topic: Axhibit Interviewee: Song Ki-seong Context: AX Day 7th Format: Interactive HTML Draft
Axhibit is not a tool that simply asks “Is the design pretty?” If an advertising image is shown to the public in advance, it is more of a tool to ask which age group will be attracted to it, which target group will ignore it, and what kind of signal the difference will provide in judging the productability.
Ceal“If you could pick just one scene where this product became clear, where would it be?”

The starting point was between the Nemotron interview and the Dieter Rams-style design review.

The idea for Axhibit didn't start with a list of features. While talking about the Nemotron interview that Jaeho had previously created, the experience of personally using the “Design Review Site from the Perspective of Dieter Rams,” which Kisung had introduced in a recent design study, naturally led to this.

What caught my eye during that experience was that it was not a method of evaluating the entire screen in a lumpy manner. When a user pointed out a specific area, it was structured to review that area in detail from an expert's perspective. A way of saying, “Users may be confused in this part,” rather than “It’s okay overall.” Axhibit's first axis was captured here.

A good design review is more of a discovery rather than an opinion piece. First, we find problems that users have overlooked and make them understand why they are problems.
Ceal“What form is expert feedback most useful for?”

Experts point out ‘problems’. However, not as a judgment but as an explanation.

Ki Seong-nim viewed the core of expert feedback as “pointing out problems.” This is because general users often pass by design problems without noticing them. The value of an expert lies in first catching the invisible problem.

However, the experts that Axhibit aims for are not sharp judges. If you only conclude, “This part is not good,” it is difficult for users to know what they need to learn. The tone set in the interview is closer to a coaching type that calmly explains the reason. Trust is created only when the cause of the problem and its impact on the user are shown.

Less helpful reviews

Feedback that only leaves judgment

There is a conclusion such as “the main button is weak,” but it is difficult for the user to know why the problem is and what criteria to look at.

Axhibit download review

Feedback that points out causes and effects together

Leave an understandable explanation like, “Key actions aren’t prominent enough on the first screen, so users may be confused about what to do next.”

The results screen should also follow this principle. The first thing the user sees is a short one-line summary of the overall evaluation. However, that one line should not be a grade table, but a “diagnosis that objectively points out one of the most important problems in simple language.” Below, the cause of the problem and its impact on users are explained in detail.

Ceal“What should be the difference between expert feedback and virtual public feedback?”

The public doesn't analyze design. I saw it for the first time, glanced at it briefly, and passed it on.

The second pillar of Axhibit is virtual public feedback. The important thing here is not to make expert feedback and public feedback the same language. Experts analyze “why this design is problematic.” The virtual public, on the other hand, must react entirely from the perspective of an ordinary person.

Public feedback is closer to first impressions and first impressions than from a design perspective. Words like “I don’t know what the screen is doing,” “I think I’ll skip it because it looks like an advertisement,” and “It looks pretty, but I’m not sure what the product is” actually become key data. This is because advertisements are not documents that are read for a long time, but rather materials that diverge interest, understanding, and desire to purchase in a short period of time.

Therefore, advertising design images, especially SNS feed advertising images, are natural representative materials. It is a format that many people intuitively look at and pass on, and the difference in first impressions and reactions by target is immediately revealed in the product value.

Ceal“Would it be enough to look at the reaction as the opinion of one or two people?”

The key is not a few comments, but the probability of a response in units of 10,000 people.

The most important turning point in the interview was here. Public reaction should not end with “what a few fictional characters said.” Ki Seong-nim pointed out that advertising decisions are not made based on one or two judgments from the public. The purpose is to confirm marketability. In other words, it looks at the possibility of purchase desire or interest.

The public feedback that Axhibit needs to create is close to a structure that collects responses from 10,000 or 100,000 people and views them at the same rate of preference. This is to probabilistically determine the likelihood that this advertisement will receive good feedback from the actual public. Therefore, the center of the report is not the overall average, but the ratio of likes and dislikes for each age group and the difference.

What these reports show is not “good/bad.” Who is it working for, who is it going astray, and where should the message be changed? For example, if the likes of the product are high among those in their 20s but the unfavorability of those in their 40s is high, advertisers should reexamine their targets and media strategies rather than taking comfort in looking at the overall average.

Ceal“So what decisions should Axhibit’s results screen help you make?”

Axhibit is more of a tool to read the commercial risks of an advertisement than a tool to select a pretty design.

Through this interview, Axhibit’s direction became clearer. Expert feedback finds design problems that users missed and explains why they are problematic. Virtual public feedback shows the first impressions and reactions of ordinary people. And in materials where public reaction is important, such as advertising images, this reaction is aggregated into large-scale ratios and differences to determine marketability.

Therefore, Axhibit’s question does not stop at “Is this design pretty?” More important questions are, “Does this ad have the potential to generate interest?”, “Can it lead to a desire to purchase?”, and “Which age group or target group will the response differ?”

  • First line summary:Present one of the most important issues in simple terms and objectively like an expert.
  • Expert review:Explains the causes of problems in specific areas and their impact on users.
  • Virtual public reaction:It is presented not from a design perspective, but from a first impression, interest, and desire to purchase.
  • Aggregated metrics:Marketability is judged through the like/dislike ratio and gap by age group.
Judgment criteria

Stop and think for a moment

Advertisement design A received an overall favorable rating of 62%. However, 78% of people in their 20s have a favorable opinion, and 34% of people in their 40s have a favorable opinion. What should advertisers check first after seeing these results?

Looking at the overall favorability rating alone, it may seem like a “good advertisement,” but from Axhibit’s perspective, the age gap is a more important signal. First, you need to check who the actual target of this advertisement is. If your core target is people in their 20s, you can push your strengths further, but if your product needs to persuade people in their 40s, you need to reexamine the message, trust factors, and utility explanation.
Ceal“What is one sentence that your colleagues who read this should take with them to their next product discussion?”

Design evaluation should not be about appreciation, but rather a signal for the next decision.

The interesting point about Axhibit is that it brings in experts and the public at the same time, but does not mix the two roles. Experts explain the cause of the problem, and the public shows their reaction when they actually see it. And the response turns into aggregated possibilities rather than individual comments.

If this structure works well, Axhibit can go beyond a design review tool and become an experimental tool that quickly reads the commercial risks of advertising and design proposals. Teams can see “where the problems are” and “who is likely to respond” before releasing the draft. At that time, the design review is not a battle of tastes, but becomes the basis for the next decision.

The core sentences of Axhibit can be summarized like this. “Before asking if it’s pretty, let’s first see who will want to buy it.”
