What to cache and what to engrave on your body

May 28, 2026 최정혁
Ceal-terview · AX sharing session

What to cache,
What to engrave on your body

Jeonghyeok Choi’s 5/28 AX sharing event announcement‘Locality and my own cache’Based on this, we re-read productivity not as simple automation, but as a sense of choosing “what to keep close” and “what to intentionally pass through.”

Presenter: Jeonghyeok Choi Source: 2026-05-28 AX Share Meeting Topic: locality · cache · LLM delegated judgment

This article is not a collection of tips that stop at “Write your clipboard history.” The point that Jeong Hyeok brought up in his presentation and interview is broader. The command we just wrote, the sentence we just copied, and the conversation we just opened retain the flow of our current work. Rather than putting it deep in a drawer every time, keep it close to your desk. Jeong Hyeok said this in CSlocality of referenceWow, explained with Cathy's sense.

Ceal asked.

Even though it was a hastily chosen topic, why did this presentation feel like something you could talk about right away, Jeonghyeok?

Jeong Hyeok's first answer was surprisingly honest from the start of the presentation. The topic was decided in a hurry. The original presenter, Hwidong, had difficulty presenting the day before, and Jeonghyeok was asked to present in his stead. However, just because it was a hasty project did not mean that it was empty-handed.

In the week before that, Jeong Hyeok had already been giving tips such as clipboard management and shell command history management to several colleagues. “Now, let’s summarize this part. This is a mental model that I have maintained for a long time, so I think I can prepare it quickly and still make it nutritious.” The presentation came suddenly, but the materials were already on the workbench.

Key Sentence Candidates
Recent commands and clipboard history are close traces of the thoughts I just passed through.

Reading locality as a business tool

Ceal asked.

What was the one action you wanted your colleagues to take immediately after hearing your presentation?

The most specific action recommended by Jeong Hyeok is that each person has his/her ownClipboard historyThe idea was to install the function and use it. Tools can be options such as Raycast, Alfred, or Hammerspoon. On the shell side, history search and rewriting of previous commands are performed.hstr, familiarctrl+rThe same habits are in the same series.

The value of this habit goes a little deeper than “makes copy and paste faster.” Recently copied text and recently entered commands retain the context of what you were just doing. The current work is largely connected to the previous work. In that case, it is natural to leave the traces so that they can be retrieved from a nearby place, rather than having to find them again every time.

Good to keep close by

  • The shell command you just entered
  • Recently copied text and links
  • AI chat context just exchanged
  • Close records of Slack, Notion, and Figma that are connected to current work

changes to expect

  • Do not repeat the same search
  • Do not interrupt the flow of work
  • View just-passed thoughts as reusable clues
  • Create a small cache hierarchy that works for you

short judgment exercise

But you shouldn't cache everything

Ceal asked.

What criteria do you use to judge the “exceptions that require deliberate finger-timing” in the original text of the presentation?

This is where the direction of the writing takes an important turn. At first, the expression “cache for me” seems to center around putting everything into nearby storage and quickly getting it out. However, Jeong Hyeok clearly made an exception. While learning the UI or grasping the mental model, there are times when you have to intentionally go through it by hand.

The scene with Jeong Hyeok istmuxIt was. I could have told LLM, “Split the window like this.” However, he deliberately went through the list of shortcut keys a few times and adjusted them manually.ctrl+bThe purpose was to learn the feeling of starting with . As long as you get results, LLM is fast. However, in order to get your hands on the workspace, there are times when a slow traversal is necessary.

Corrected Contrast
Command history and clipboard history are “close traces.” However, what you need to learn in your body and thinking is not to cache, but to properly engrave.

What to entrust to an LLM and what not to entrust to an LLM

Ceal asked.

What is the standard for distinguishing between tasks that must be handled on purpose rather than being left to the LLM, and tasks that can be simply ordered to the LLM?

Jeonghyeok’s standards were simple. If it's something you're already good at or something you don't plan on making your own right away, you can ask LLM to do it. On the other hand, things that need to be mastered, things that need to be developed in muscle memory, and things that need to be studied and understood are deliberately done step by step by oneself, or even asked the LLM to “do it for me.”

This standard is not about reducing the use of LLM. Rather, it is closer to a standard for better delegation. To know what can be left to external tools, you must know what should remain within yourself. The expression “engrave” that Jeong Hyeok mentioned is important here. Cache is a nearby storage that is quickly accessed and used. Engraving is something that is slowly and repeatedly put into the basic layers of the body and thoughts.

Jeong Hyeok mainly used the word “engraving” to mean repetition that becomes familiar. However, the repetition does not end with simple mechanical training. As fluency in repeatedly handling pieces increases, it ultimately leads to higher-level understanding. In other words, the act of memorizing shortcuts changes the way you view the workspace at some point.

quiet checkpoint

Think of one task I would like to entrust to my LLM today. Is this work “something I already know or don’t need to make into my own right away,” or is it “something I need to carve into my own hands, even if it’s slow”?

As a standard for this article, I look at “whether it should remain within me” before speed. If you only need the result and it doesn't have a big impact on your next decision, it's okay to delegate. On the other hand, if it is a mental model of the basic operations, mindset, and tools that you will frequently deal with in the future, going through it yourself at least once will create greater fluency in the long run.

Productivity is not achieved through delegation skills alone.

Ceal is knotted

Is the conclusion of this article closer to “Distinguish between what to entrust to an LLM and what to engrave yourself” or is it closer to “Intentionally repeat inefficiently what needs to be engraved”?

Jeong Hyeok asked the interviewer to conclude this part in a sensible way. So, rather than choosing just one of the two, this article connects the two sentences.

Productivity in the AI era comes not from the ability to delegate everything, but from the sense of choosing what not to delegate.

Not delegating doesn't mean rejecting tools. Whether or not to pursue an LLM is not a question of abstinence. The standard is, “Can this ability be placed outside of me, or should it be engraved within my body and thoughts?” You can leave the work to LLM that you are already good at or that you don't need to make your own right now. Buttmuxofctrl+bLike, when it comes to creating a sense of my work space, taking a slow path for a while actually makes for a faster path later.

Locality and Cathy tell us to “keep past thoughts close.” As for Jeong Hyeok’s exception, one sentence is added here. “Keep close what needs to be kept close, but don’t pass by too quickly what needs to be engraved.”

A little experiment you can try right away

  • Turn on a clipboard history tool and throughout the day record the moments when you retrieve and rewrite “what you just copied.”
  • Consciously use shell command history search and reduce the number of commands you enter repeatedly.
  • Before asking LLM, stop for 10 seconds and ask. “Do I just need a result for this, or should I engrave it on my hand?”
